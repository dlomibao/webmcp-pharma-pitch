The definition
02 / 11
If you remember nothing else
WebMCP is a way for a brand's website to publish a list of approved answers that AI assistants like ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude can read directly — instead of guessing or pulling from random sources.
That's it. Everything else is detail.
Practice saying this out loud. If you stumble, read it again.
02 / 11
This is the slide everyone needs to internalize. Read the sentence aloud. Then say: "That sentence is your whole job. If a client asks 'what's WebMCP,' you say that sentence. Don't improvise. Don't add jargon. Don't add 'well actually' caveats." If your team looks tense "Look — we don't need to be technical experts. We need to be content experts who happen to have a new content surface to publish to. That's it."
The three pillars
03 / 11
Own these or don't go in

Three things — say them in your own words before you walk in.

01
It's content work — not engineering.
The brand publishes a small set of structured answers (dosing, interactions, side effects) on their existing website. AI assistants read those when patients ask.
02
It's the same family as things you already do.
Schema.org tags, OpenGraph snippets, ALT text — the whole structured-content-for-machines world. WebMCP is in that family. The audience is just new (AI assistants).
03
The agency owns it.
Brand IT does a few hours of plumbing — they wire up one URL. Everything else — questions, answers, MLR cycle, updates — is the agency's content workflow.
If you can deliver these three points without notes, you can deliver the WebMCP slide.
03 / 11
Make people say these out loud during training. Not read — say. In their own words. "Pick a pillar. Now explain it to me like I'm a pharma CMO who's never heard of WebMCP. Go." Common stumble People skip pillar 3 because it feels self-promotional. It's not — it's the answer to "is this our agency or a new vendor?" If they don't say "agency owns it," the client routes the work to their IT vendor and we lose the project.
The most common trap
04 / 11
Read this twice. Memorize the deploy line.

Someone in the meeting will get tripped up on "MCP."

◆ MCP
Model Context Protocol
A protocol that AI applications use to pull internal data inward — connecting an AI app to internal databases, tools, documents. Backend infrastructure. Built by IT/engineering teams.
◇ WebMCP
Web Model Context Protocol
The same naming family applied at the browser. Lets a public website publish content outward — declaring what it'll answer and the answer. Public-facing content. Built and owned by the agency / brand team.
Sentence to deploy
"Common confusion — they share a name because they share principles, but MCP is your backend, WebMCP is your front door. This conversation is only about the front door."
If anyone insists on technical depth, the deck has a backup slide (B6) for it. Don't go deeper unprompted.
04 / 11
This is the single most likely reason the meeting falls apart. "A semi-technical person in the room will hear 'MCP,' connect it to a backend protocol they vaguely remember from an Anthropic article, and decide this is engineering's problem. The whole conversation routes to IT. We lose the project to a system integrator." "The deploy line is your one tool. Memorize it. Front door, not backend. Say it once, move on. Don't argue about it." If it gets challenged Don't defend the protocol distinction technically. Just keep returning to: "the WebMCP work IS content work. The agency does it. Brand IT does a few hours of plumbing." If you're really stuck Punt to the follow-up. "Great question — let's bring our technical lead and your digital team into the same room next week and we'll walk through the architectural side."
Trap question · 1 of 3
05 / 11
When the IT-aware person speaks up
Isn't this just an API?
Acknowledge → reframe → metaphor Yes, technically. But that framing is unhelpful and feels engineering-y. Steer back to the menu metaphor — that's what makes it land for non-technical people.
Say this
"In the technical sense, yes. But for your team it works exactly like any other content workflow — your agency writes approved answers and publishes them. The technical wiring is a few hours of one-time setup. Everything that matters happens in the existing MLR cycle."
After delivering — pivot back: "Your website hands the AI a menu of questions it can answer."
05 / 11
The trap: confirming "yes it's an API" without reframing makes the client mentally route the work to engineering. The fix: confirm the technical fact, immediately pivot to "it works like any content workflow." "Don't deny the technical truth. Acknowledge it, then bridge: 'but for your team it works like any content cycle.' Then go back to the menu metaphor — that's the picture that sticks."
Trap question · 2 of 3
06 / 11
The conversation-killer the deck is built to prevent
We'd need to talk to our IT vendor about this.
If you let this stand, you lose the project This is the worst outcome. The client routes the conversation to a system integrator who has no relationship with the brand and no MLR experience. By the time it comes back to you, the scope has tripled and the agency is the subcontractor. Don't let it stand.
Push back gently
"Actually no — that's the great part. Brand IT does a few hours of plumbing once. Everything that matters — the questions, the answers, the MLR cycle, the updates — is content work that your agency does. We're already set up for it. There's no new vendor to bring in."
Don't be combative — be confident. The client is offering a path of perceived safety; show them the agency-led path is actually safer.
06 / 11
This is the single highest-stakes moment of the meeting. If the client mentally hands this off to IT, the project is gone — or worse, becomes a 6-month integration with no MLR insight. "Don't argue. Don't get defensive. Just confidently restate: 'this is content. The plumbing is trivial. Your agency does the work that matters — the same MLR cycle you already trust us with.'" If they push back "Happy to bring our technical lead to the next conversation if it would help your IT team get comfortable. But the bulk of this — the part with budget and timeline — sits with us."
Trap question · 3 of 3
07 / 11
When asked for technical depth you don't have
How do you actually build this? What are the steps?
Do not improvise an answer You will get this question. Do not improvise a technical answer. Don't guess at JSON, schemas, deployment steps, or anything technical. You will get something wrong, the room will notice, and credibility evaporates.
Punt cleanly
"That's the right question for the follow-up — when we walk through the scope on a real brand, we'll bring our technical lead and your digital team into the same room. Today is just to land the concept."
Punting to a follow-up with the right people is more credible than fumbling a technical answer.
07 / 11
The instinct is to prove you know what you're talking about by trying to answer. Resist it. Punting is the strong move. "You're not the technical person in this conversation. You're the strategy person. The technical answer comes from someone whose job it is to give it. You'll set up that conversation, but not have it yourself." If they keep pushing "I want to give you a precise answer rather than approximate one. Let me get our technical lead on the follow-up call this week."
The forbidden phrases
08 / 11
Cuts the meeting short, tanks credibility

Don't say these — even if you think you can pull it off.

⊘
"Schema markup" without defining it. Most clients don't know what it is. Say "structured content tags" — gesture at it as familiar without making them prove they know.
⊘
"JSON," "API endpoint," "tool registry." The client glazes over. Lose the technical vocabulary; the deck doesn't use it.
⊘
Quote the protocol spec. If you find yourself referencing W3C section numbers, you've lost the room.
⊘
Promise specific browser timelines beyond "Chrome today, Edge likely soon (Microsoft co-authored)." Anything more specific is speculation.
⊘
Quote a price. Say: "Happy to scope that in the follow-up — depends on the brand and how your MLR workflow is set up today."
⊘
Apologize for the fictional drug. The disclaimer is on the slide; the room sees it. Apologizing implies it's a problem you're conceding.
Read this once before any meeting. Then read it again.
08 / 11
Walk through each one in training. For each, ask the team: "Have you said this in a meeting before? What happened?" Most of these are habits picked up from selling other products. Naming them out loud helps unlearn them. The hardest to break "Schema markup" is the hardest because it feels like the right comparison. It is the right comparison — but the client has to recognize it for the metaphor to work. Half don't. Use "structured content tags" instead and the metaphor lands universally.

The escape hatches
10 / 11
It's OK not to know — it's not OK to fumble

When you hit a question you can't answer, say one of these.

I want to give you a precise answer on that — let me get our technical lead on the follow-up call.
That's exactly the kind of detail we'd work through in scope. Today's meeting is to land the concept.
Honestly, I'd rather not guess at that. Let me get back to you with a real answer this week.
The client trusts you more for saying you don't know than for fumbling. They've seen confident-and-wrong before. Be the confident-and-honest one.
Pick one in the moment. Don't string two together — that sounds evasive.
10 / 11
Train this with role-play: hit the team with hard questions and have them deliver one of these three lines without hesitation. "The reason this works is that pharma execs deal with internal IT and consultants who BS them constantly. Saying 'I don't know, let me get you a real answer' breaks pattern. It's almost disarming. Use it." What NOT to do Don't apologize repeatedly. One escape hatch. One follow-up commitment. Move on.
