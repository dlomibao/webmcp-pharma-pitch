# WebMCP — team primer

> **Internal only. Do not send to clients.**
>
> Read this 5 minutes before you walk into the meeting. It's the floor of what you need to know. If you can't say the **bold sentences** in your own words, ask someone before you go in.

---

## What WebMCP is — in one sentence

**WebMCP is a way for a brand's website to publish a list of approved answers that AI assistants like ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude can read directly — instead of guessing or pulling from random sources.**

That's it. Everything else is detail.

---

## Three things to remember

1. **It's content work, not engineering.** The brand publishes a small set of structured answers ("here's the dosing info, here's the interactions list, here's the side effects") on their existing website. AI assistants read those answers when patients ask questions.

2. **It's the same family as things you already do.** Schema.org tags, OpenGraph snippets, the ALT text on images — that whole world of "structured content for machines." WebMCP is in that family. The audience is just new (AI assistants instead of search crawlers).

3. **The agency owns it.** Brand IT does a few hours of plumbing — they wire up one URL on the website. Everything else — what questions to publish, what the answers say, the MLR cycle, updates as new data lands — is the agency's content workflow. Same as everything else you do.

---

## The MCP confusion — read this twice

**Someone in the meeting will get tripped up on "MCP."** They've heard of it. They might think it's a backend engineering thing. Here's what's actually true:

- **MCP** (Model Context Protocol) is a protocol AI applications use to **pull internal data** — like connecting an AI app to a company database. Backend infrastructure. IT/engineering scope.
- **WebMCP** (Web Model Context Protocol) is the same naming family applied at the browser. It lets a public website **publish public content outward** for AI assistants to read.

**The one sentence to deploy if it comes up:**

> *"Common confusion — they share a name because they share design principles, but MCP is your backend infrastructure and WebMCP is your front door. This conversation is only about the front door."*

Then move on. Don't get pulled into a technical compare-and-contrast. The deck has a backup slide (B6) that explains this in detail if anyone insists on the depth.

---

## What to say if a client asks "isn't this just an API?"

Yes, technically. But that framing is unhelpful and feels engineering-y. Better:

> *"In the technical sense, yes. But for your team it works exactly like any other content workflow — your agency writes approved answers and publishes them. The technical wiring is a few hours of one-time setup. Everything that matters happens in the existing MLR cycle."*

Then steer back to the menu metaphor: **"Your website hands the AI a menu of questions it can answer."**

---

## What to say if derailed into "we'd need to talk to our IT vendor"

This is the conversation-killer the deck is designed to prevent. Here's the rebuttal:

> *"Actually no — that's the great part. Brand IT does a few hours of plumbing once. Everything that matters — the questions, the answers, the MLR cycle, the updates — is content work that your agency does. We're already set up for it. There's no new vendor to bring in."*

---

## What to say if asked "how do you actually do this?"

Do not improvise a technical answer. Say:

> *"That's the right question for the follow-up conversation — when we walk through the scope on a real brand, we'll bring our technical lead and your digital team into the same room. Today is just to land the concept."*

---

## What NOT to say

- **Don't** say "schema markup" without defining it (most clients don't know what it is either — say "structured content tags," gesture at it as a familiar idea without making them prove they know what it is).
- **Don't** say "JSON" or "API endpoint" or "tool registry" — the client glazes over.
- **Don't** quote the protocol spec.
- **Don't** promise specific browser timelines beyond "Chrome today, Edge likely soon (Microsoft co-authored)."
- **Don't** quote a price. Say *"Happy to scope that in the follow-up — depends on the brand and how your MLR workflow is set up today."*
- **Don't** apologize for the fictional drug. The disclaimer is on the slide; the room sees it.

---

## The meeting flow, summarized

1. Hook on the 40M number — set up that AI is already in your patients' lives
2. Show traffic collapse — the problem is structural, not cyclical
3. Show the AI is talking about your drug whether you participate or not
4. Introduce WebMCP — *content work, not infrastructure, agency owns it*
5. Show MLR inversion — compliance is on the inside now, not chasing
6. Tee up Veritrax — fictional drug, here's why
7. **Run the demo** — this does the persuasion. Don't over-narrate.
8. Soft close — ask for a second meeting with their digital + MLR leads

If you forget everything else: **the demo is the persuasion. Your job is to set it up well and not screw up the WebMCP framing.**

## If you're asked something you don't know

Say one of:
- *"I want to give you a precise answer on that — let me get our [tech lead / MLR specialist / strategist] on the follow-up call."*
- *"That's exactly the kind of detail we'd work through in scope. Today's meeting is to land the concept."*
- *"Honestly, I'd rather not guess at that. Let me get back to you with a real answer this week."*

The client trusts you more for saying you don't know than for fumbling.
