The new layer
In plain English

WebMCP. This is content work — not infrastructure.

What it actually is

Your website hands every AI assistant a menu of questions it can answer — with the exact, MLR-approved answer for each.

  • Same family as schema.org tags, OpenGraph snippets, and ALT text — structured content for machines, written by the same teams that already write your other content.
  • Not a chatbot. Not a new website. Not an IT project.
  • Built by Google + Microsoft at the W3C. Live in Chrome's early-preview channel today.

Who does what

Brand IT
One URL on your existing site. A few hours of plumbing.
Your agency
Decides which questions to publish. Writes the MLR-approved answers. Owns the cycle. Updates as new data lands.
AI assistants
Read what you publish. Quote it. Cite your brand.
Sources: W3C Community Group draft (Feb 10, 2026); Chrome 146 release notes & developer blog; VentureBeat / Search Engine Land coverage Feb–Mar 2026.

Q1 · Browser coverage
"Only Chrome — what about the others?"

Short answer Microsoft co-authored the spec. Edge support is highly likely. Other browsers haven't announced timelines.

The pattern It mirrors how schema.org and OpenGraph rolled out — one browser shipped first, the rest followed within 12-18 months once adoption proved out.

Even Chrome-only is ~65% of US browser share. Plus Chrome's engine (Chromium) powers Edge, Brave, Opera, and a long tail of others. The functional reach is significantly higher than the Chrome share alone.

Don't pretend other browsers are confirmed. They aren't. The honest framing IS the persuasive one — Microsoft co-authoring is unusually strong signal, and Chromium reach is what's actually relevant.
Q2 · Regulatory exposure
"Doesn't publishing a structured tool list make us a bigger FDA target?"

Short answer No — and arguably the opposite.

Why The content in the WebMCP layer is the same MLR-approved content that already lives on your site. Structuring it doesn't change what's said — it changes how reliably it's surfaced.

The flip Today, the FDA is using AI to find non-compliant claims in your unstructured content. WebMCP makes your content easier to audit — for you and for them. That's a feature for compliance, not a bug.

This is the question MLR will ask. Stay calm. The answer is genuinely strong because the underlying content doesn't change — only its discoverability does. If they push, offer to set up the next conversation specifically with their compliance lead.
Q3 · Determinism
"Can the AI still paraphrase or wrap unapproved commentary around our answer?"

Honest answer WebMCP guarantees the payload — the actual medical content — is served exactly as you wrote it. The AI surface (ChatGPT, Gemini, etc.) controls the framing language around it.

In practice Most current implementations cite the source verbatim when a tool returns structured medical content. This is a platform policy decision driven by liability — not us, not the protocol. Liability is pushing the platforms in a more verbatim direction, not less.

The 80/20 framing WebMCP solves about 80% of the determinism problem. The remaining 20% is platform policy. That's better than what you have today, which is 0% controlled.

Don't overclaim. The 80/20 framing is honest and more credible than promising perfect determinism. If they want to see a paraphrasing example, the demo's MLR overlay shows exactly what gets locked vs. what the AI surface is doing around it.
Q4 · Commitment & cost
"What does this commit us to? What if we want out?"

Short answer Nothing, and you can take it down at any time.

What it actually is Small text files served from your existing website. No new platform. No new vendor lock. No replatform. No migration. It bolts onto whatever CMS you already use.

Path back Take down the menu, take down the answers, AI behavior reverts to today's behavior. No data trapped, no contracts to unwind.

Procurement-style question. The answer is genuinely "nothing" — that's the honest case. Don't undersell that. It's a critical de-risking point.
Q5 · Timing
"Why now? Why not wait 12 months until it's stable?"

Short answer AIs preferentially cite sources they've successfully queried before. There's a recency-and-reliability flywheel.

What that means in practice Brands that publish during the 2026 preview window become the default citation for their therapy class as adoption widens. Fast followers get referenced sometimes. Late movers don't.

The history rhymes Same dynamic played out with Google snippets in 2014, with schema.org markup in 2011, and with mobile-first indexing in 2018. Brands that moved during the preview window kept the share. Brands that waited didn't catch up.

The "wait and see" objection feels safe but is the most expensive choice. Use the historical pattern — the room will recognize at least one of those examples. Don't get drawn into citing exact share-shift numbers; the pattern is what's persuasive.
Q6 · The naming confusion
"Wait — is this the MCP I've heard of? The backend thing?"

Short answer Different things. Same naming family. Opposite directions.

MCP (Model Context Protocol) A protocol that AI applications use to pull data inward — connecting an AI app to internal databases, internal tools, internal documents. Backend infrastructure built and run by IT/engineering teams.

WebMCP (Web Model Context Protocol) The same naming family, applied at the browser. Lets a public website publish content outward — declaring "here's what I'll answer, and here's the answer." Public-facing content built and owned by the agency / brand team that already runs the site.

The one-line distinction to remember MCP pulls private data in. WebMCP publishes public content out. They share a name because they share design principles — they don't share scope, owners, or budget.

Most common confusion in any room. The trap: someone hears "MCP" and assumes IT scope, derails the meeting into "let's get our engineering team involved." Head it off proactively if the team or client looks puzzled at the WebMCP slide. Quick sentence to deploy "Common confusion — they share a name because they share principles, but MCP is your backend, WebMCP is your front door. This conversation is only about the front door."
